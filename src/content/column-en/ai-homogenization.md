## Why "anyone can write well now" became an HR and education problem

Entrance-exam essays, internal proposals, consulting deliverables, school reports — for a long time, "writing ability" was one of the few measurable windows into the quality of someone's thinking. Then ChatGPT, Claude, and their peers spread, and we entered an era where almost anyone can reach the upper edge of textual quality. Producing polished prose at scale is no longer an individual differentiator.

The harder question begins here. Once AI assistance has flattened surface quality, how does an organization decide which person can actually think independently? For HR, education businesses, learning and development, and recruiting leaders, the traditional framework of "did they write well?" is rapidly losing its evaluative force.

Two studies released in 2026 [^1] [^2] tackled this issue head-on. One proposes a framework that quantifies the rarity of arguments. The other shows, through a large-scale experiment, how much AI assistance homogenizes student thinking. This column re-reads the pair as decision-making material for management and talent leaders.

## Three facts the research established

### 1. "Writing quality" and "originality of thought" can be measured as separate things

AROA (Argument Rarity-based Originality Assessment) is a framework that scores essay originality along four axes — structural rarity, claim rarity, evidence rarity, and reasoning depth. The crucial point is that these are designed as indicators independent of grammatical correctness or logical coherence.

In a comparison of 1,375 human-written essays and 1,000 AI-generated essays, the AI essays reached writing-quality scores comparable to the top tier of human writers, while their claim rarity sat at roughly one-fifth of the human baseline. In other words, "the ability to write well" and "the ability to raise points others do not" can no longer be substituted for one another by a single metric.

### 2. AI assistance lifts quality, but strips diversity from argument structure

The second study compared 6,875 essays across five conditions (fully human, no AI, light AI assistance, heavy AI assistance, AI-only generation) and simultaneously measured writing quality and the diversity of argumentative structure.

The result: as AI assistance intensifies, writing quality rises clearly, but variance in argument structure (the patterns connecting claims to evidence) drops by 68 to 78 percent. The paper calls this the "quality-homogenization trade-off."

Translated into business terms: AI assistance lets everyone produce "above-average prose," yet at the organizational level, the risk that "everyone ends up thinking alike" has now been quantitatively confirmed.

### 3. Surface quality no longer reveals who actually thought for themselves

A further implication is that, when "good writing" is the evaluation standard, you can no longer tell apart an applicant who outsourced to AI from one who reasoned independently.

If admissions or recruiting reviewers look only at writing quality, they raise their risk of overlooking the very candidates they most want — those who can stake out original positions. This is a fundamental selection-process design issue.

## What this research tells the business leader

Three takeaways.

1. Writing quality has become a "leveled prerequisite" in the AI-assisted era and has lost its power as a differentiator.
2. What organizations actually want is people who can raise original points, and that cannot be measured by quality alone. Other axes — rarity and reasoning depth — are now necessary.
3. An organization in which everyone uses AI and no one thinks independently loses its argumentative diversity. This connects directly to medium- and long-term decision-quality risk.

From here, action across talent, education, and assessment is needed.

---

## Risk management: three areas to act on now

### Risk 1: Recruiting — score essays by "rarity," not by "quality"

Companies that grade applicant essays on readability, structure, and absence of typos are now throwing away most of their differentiation signal. Anyone using AI can clear that bar.

What to do: rewrite recruiting prompts from "questions you can score by writing well" to "questions only score if you bring a point, counterexample, or experience others did not." Add "claim rarity" and "evidence originality" as explicit axes in the evaluation rubric.

### Risk 2: Talent development — over-relying on AI atrophies the "original-point muscle"

When AI tools are released too freely in junior-talent training, the experience of building an original line of argument is lost. The research finding that AI assistance correlates inversely with argument-structure diversity matters here.

What to do: deliberately design "no-AI" stretches into training programs. At minimum, mandate that the early phases — problem framing and hypothesis formation — be done without AI. Reserve AI for the "polishing" phase.

### Risk 3: Organizational collective intelligence — same tool, thinner debate

Meeting decks, proposals, consulting deliverables — when all internal documents flow from the same generative tool, organizational argument patterns converge. The short-term efficiency gain comes with long-term erosion of innovation seeds.

What to do: for important decision documents, require explicit declaration of how much AI was used and where. Make the "AI draft → human review" process visible so that original-thought zones are protected.

## Value creation: three opportunities to take the other way around

### Opportunity 1: Recruiting platforms with rarity scoring built in

The rarity-quantification idea behind AROA is a business opportunity for HR-tech and education-tech vendors. Services that can present a "rarity score" alongside an applicant's essay could become the new baseline in AI-era hiring tools.

### Opportunity 2: Disclose "thinking diversity" as an organizational metric

Companies that can report something like an "internal-document argument diversity index" in their ESG or IR disclosures send a powerful signal to investors and high-potential talent. "We use AI, but we resist the homogenization of thinking" can become a new kind of organizational quality certificate.

### Opportunity 3: Productize "thinking without AI" training

Precisely because we live in an AI era, the market value of training that "raises original points and gathers original evidence without AI" rises. For learning and development firms and consultancies, "restoring independent thought to AI-native generations" can become a new revenue line.

## Closing — from "writes well" to "thinks originally" as the thing we measure

AI flattening writing quality is also an opportunity to make the substance of evaluation visible. What organizations actually wanted was never "people who write well" — it was "people who think originally." Writing quality was merely a proxy.

The research shows quantitatively that the proxy no longer works. It also shows that new dimensions — rarity and reasoning depth — are technically measurable. For HR, education, and assessment design, this is the moment to rethink "what do we actually measure?" from the ground up.

The next time you sit down to redesign an essay rubric, I hope this column's checklist comes to mind.
